2026 California Fire Code Changes: What NFPA 72’s 2025 Edition Means for LA Commercial Buildings

California now enforces the 2025 edition of NFPA 72 through the 2025 California Fire Code, effective January 1, 2026. Here is what changed, how Los Angeles-area authorities are interpreting it, and the steps commercial building owners should take to stay compliant.

Get a 2026-Compliance Quote

If you own or manage a commercial property in Los Angeles, the San Fernando Valley, Torrance, or Santa Monica, your fire alarm inspection, testing, and maintenance (ITM) program is now evaluated against a new rulebook. The 2025 edition of NFPA 72, the National Fire Alarm and Signaling Code, has been adopted into the 2025 California Fire Code (Title 24, Part 9) and is enforced statewide beginning January 1, 2026. Los Angeles County’s own fire code update took effect the same day. This guide breaks down the changes that actually affect commercial owners, the cost implications, and what your authority having jurisdiction (AHJ) will be looking for in 2026.

Effective dates and adoption

California updates its building and fire codes on a three-year cycle. The 2025 California Fire Code incorporates NFPA 72, 2025 edition, by reference. The statewide effective date for enforcement is January 1, 2026. In the Los Angeles area, the Los Angeles County Fire Code also became effective January 1, 2026, and individual cities adopt the state baseline with local amendments on or around the same date. In practice, any ITM visit, plan review, or new installation evaluated in 2026 is held to the 2025 edition rather than the prior 2022-cycle rules.

The changes that matter most for commercial owners

1. Cybersecurity for network-connected systems

The 2025 edition places new emphasis on cybersecurity for fire alarm and signaling systems that rely on network connectivity. As more commercial fire alarm panels communicate over building IT networks and the internet, the code reinforces the need to protect them from unauthorized access and cyber threats. For owners, this means your contractor should be able to document access management and basic threat protection for any networked system — not just confirm that the panel powers on and reports.

2. Supervisory device testing moves to semiannual

One of the most concrete operational changes: functional testing of certain supervisory devices — notably waterflow switches and valve tamper switches — has increased in frequency under the 2025 edition. Where many buildings previously tested these annually, the new cycle is semiannual. This is the change most likely to show up on your invoice, typically adding roughly $200 to $800 per building per year depending on how many devices you have.

3. Enhanced monitoring and dual-path communication

The 2025 updates push many commercial systems toward enhanced monitoring capabilities, with newer systems expected to support improved communication pathways including dual-path monitoring. The principle is redundancy: if one communication line (for example, a cellular path) fails, a second path takes over so the central station never loses contact with the building. Owners with older single-path communicators should ask whether an upgrade is advisable or required for their occupancy.

4. Restricted Audible Mode Operation (RAMO)

The 2025 edition expands guidance on Restricted Audible Mode Operation, which allows reduced notification sound levels in specific environments such as schools, healthcare facilities, and certain special-occupancy spaces. RAMO is not a free pass to turn down the horns: using it requires approval from the AHJ and must be supported by a documented risk analysis. If your facility is considering RAMO, plan for the approval and documentation steps.

5. Documentation and responsibility clarifications

The 2025 code refines documentation requirements and clarifies who is responsible for design, inspection, testing, and maintenance. The intent is consistency and accountability across a building’s fire alarm program. The practical takeaway for owners: complete, well-organized ITM records are more important than ever, and gaps in record-keeping are an easy finding for an inspector to write up.

How Los Angeles-area AHJs enforce it

There is no single “LA fire department.” Which authority enforces the code on your building depends on where it sits:

  • City of Los Angeles — the Los Angeles Fire Department (LAFD) is the AHJ, including most of the San Fernando Valley within city limits (Van Nuys, Sherman Oaks, Studio City, Northridge, and similar neighborhoods).
  • Unincorporated LA County and contract cities — the Los Angeles County Fire Department enforces the LA County Fire Code.
  • Santa Monica — Santa Monica Fire Department (SMFD).
  • Torrance — Torrance Fire Department (TFD).
  • Burbank and Glendale — their respective city fire departments (BFD, GFD), which cover the parts of the Valley outside city of LA limits.

All of these enforce the 2025 California Fire Code as the baseline, but each can add local amendments and each runs its own permit, plan-review, and re-inspection process. When in doubt about a specific requirement, your fire alarm contractor should confirm the interpretation with the AHJ that actually has jurisdiction over your address.

What the changes mean for your budget

For most commercial buildings, the 2026 changes are a modest annual increase rather than a major capital event. The semiannual supervisory testing is the main recurring add (about $200–$800 per building per year). Buildings that need to add or upgrade dual-path communication face a one-time equipment-and-labor cost. Networked systems may incur a little extra review time for cybersecurity documentation. For context, full commercial fire alarm ITM in Los Angeles ranges from roughly $300 per year for a small space with a single fire alarm control panel up to $30,000 per year for a high-rise with 200+ devices and emergency responder communication coverage — device count is the primary driver. (These are market-range figures; your actual cost depends on your building.)

A 2026 compliance checklist for owners

  • Confirm your ITM schedule reflects semiannual testing of waterflow and tamper (supervisory) devices.
  • Ask your contractor whether your monitoring meets the enhanced communication / dual-path expectations for your occupancy.
  • If your fire alarm system is on a building network, make sure cybersecurity controls are documented.
  • Keep complete, organized ITM records — the 2025 edition tightens documentation and responsibility requirements.
  • Verify which AHJ has jurisdiction over your address and whether it has local amendments.
  • Schedule your annual inspection early in the year so any deficiencies can be corrected before a re-inspection deadline.

Frequently asked questions

When does the 2025 edition of NFPA 72 take effect in California?

California adopted NFPA 72, 2025 edition through the 2025 California Fire Code (Title 24, Part 9), with enforcement beginning January 1, 2026. The Los Angeles County Fire Code update also took effect January 1, 2026. Commercial buildings inspected, tested, and maintained in 2026 are evaluated against the new edition.

What are the most important NFPA 72 2025 changes for commercial buildings?

The four with the biggest operational impact: new cybersecurity requirements for network-connected systems; semiannual functional testing of supervisory devices such as waterflow and tamper switches (previously annual); enhanced communication including dual-path monitoring for many systems; and expanded Restricted Audible Mode Operation guidance requiring AHJ approval and a documented risk analysis.

Do the 2026 changes increase my annual fire alarm testing cost?

For most commercial buildings, yes, modestly. Moving supervisory device testing from annual to semiannual typically adds about $200 to $800 per building per year depending on device count. Buildings that need dual-path communication may have a one-time equipment and labor cost, and networked systems may add cybersecurity review time.

Who enforces the new fire code on my building in the Los Angeles area?

Your local Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ): typically LAFD in the City of Los Angeles, the LA County Fire Department in unincorporated areas and contract cities, and city departments such as SMFD (Santa Monica), TFD (Torrance), Burbank Fire, and Glendale Fire. Each enforces the 2025 California Fire Code baseline and may add local amendments.

What should building owners do now to stay compliant in 2026?

Confirm your ITM schedule reflects semiannual supervisory testing; ask your contractor whether your monitoring meets the enhanced communication and dual-path expectations; document cybersecurity controls if your system is networked; and keep complete ITM records, since the 2025 edition refines documentation and responsibility requirements.
