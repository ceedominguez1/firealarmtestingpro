Fire Alarm Testing FAQs: NFPA 72 & Los Angeles Compliance

Common questions from property managers and facility teams about fire alarm inspection schedules, what's covered, and what happens during an LAFD audit.

How often does NFPA 72 require fire alarm testing in California?

Per NFPA 72 Chapter 14 and the California Fire Code, a full functional test of the entire system is required annually. Additional checks happen on shorter intervals: waterflow alarms and supervisory signals semi-annually, off-premises monitoring transmission quarterly, and emergency lighting load tests monthly (via NFPA 101). FireAlarmTestingPro tracks all of these deadlines for you. Read the full 2026 NFPA 72 frequency guide →

What does an annual fire alarm inspection actually cover?

A NICET-certified technician tests every component of your system: the control panel and power supplies, every smoke and heat detector, manual pull stations, audible horns and visual strobes, battery load capacity, off-premises monitoring transmission, and integrated systems like elevator recall, HVAC shutdown, and magnetic door holders. You receive a signed, audit-ready digital report the same day, formatted for LAFD review.

What happens if my fire alarm system fails an inspection?

Failed components are documented as deficiencies. We provide a same-visit repair quote for items we can fix on the spot (batteries, devices, programming) and a written plan for larger issues. While a deficiency exists, you may need to put a fire watch in place per LAFD or your local AHJ. Our job is to close every deficiency before your insurance audit or fire marshal visit.

Are LAFD permits or Regulation 4 paperwork part of the inspection?

If your building is inside the City of Los Angeles, LAFD enforces NFPA 72 plus its own Regulation 4 program for monitored systems. We deliver inspection reports formatted for LAFD review and coordinate with your existing permit holder where required. For new installs or modifications that need an LAFD permit, we partner with your installer or designer.

How long do I need to keep fire alarm inspection records?

NFPA 72 requires the most recent test record on-site and available to the AHJ on request. California Fire Code and most local AHJs expect at least 3 years of records retained. We deliver tamper-evident digital reports stored in your inbox, which most property managers keep indefinitely for insurance and litigation defense.
